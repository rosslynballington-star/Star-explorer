Stars

colossal spheres of hot, glowing gas—primarily hydrogen and helium—that generate energy through nuclear fusion in their cores, producing light and heat

Key Characteristics

Composition: Stars are mostly hydrogen and helium, though they also contain trace amounts of other elements.

Plasma: The immense heat and energy inside a star strip atoms of their electrons, creating a state of matter called plasma.

Gravity: A star's own gravity holds its massive form together.

Energy Generation

Nuclear Fusion: At the core of a star, intense pressure and temperature cause hydrogen atoms to fuse into helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy as light and heat.

Stellar Life Cycle

Formation: Stars form from dense regions within large clouds of gas and dust, known as nebulae, which collapse under their own gravity.

Main Sequence: Once nuclear fusion begins, a star enters its stable, main-sequence phase, burning hydrogen in its core.

Evolution and Death: As a star ages, it exhausts its hydrogen fuel. Low-mass stars, like our Sun, will eventually become red giants and then die as white dwarfs. More massive stars, however, explode in a supernova, scattering their material to form new stars and planets.

Visibility and Scale

Galactic Building Blocks: Stars are the fundamental components of galaxies, with the Milky Way alone containing billions of stars.

Immense Distance: Although some stars are visible to the naked eye, their enormous distances from Earth make them appear as tiny points of light.
